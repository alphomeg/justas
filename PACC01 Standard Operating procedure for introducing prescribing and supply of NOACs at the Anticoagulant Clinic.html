Standard Operating procedure for introducing prescribing and supply of NOACs at the Anticoagulant Clinic

Introduction

Due to the COVID emergency, it has been agreed by local commissioners and specialists to review and switch all appropriate patients over to a NOAC.

Purpose

The purpose of the SOP is to provide guidance on the process for identifying appropriate anticoagulation patients on VKA who need anticoagulant medicines review and the introduction of NOACs as an alternative treatment to VKA. The SOP will only include patients with non-valvular atrial fibrillation and a target INR range of 2 to 3. Any other indications will be excluded.

1-Procedure for Identification of patients

Process:

  1. PROCEDURE FOR IDENTIFICATION OF PATIENTS



Anticoagulant pharmacist will generate list using DAWN, CED, ICE of all patients on VKA with indications appropriate to switch.

Prioritise in terms of the following within the AF cohort

  1. Patients >70 years of age

  2. Patients shielding in line with NHS England advice

  3. Frequent clinic visits (every 7-14 days)

  4. Patients whom can provide an estimation of recent weight

  5. Immobile patients requiring domiciliary visits



In terms of locations, next priorities are

  1. Clinic (to minimise repeated site visits )

  2. Community



Whilst TTR can be used to prioritise subsequently for switching, TTR will not be a factor in deciding to exclude / include patients.

    1. Anticoagulant pharmacist will review list

Exclude the following for consideration of switch, these patients are not eligible for NOACs:

  • Hypersensitivity to the active substance or to any of the excipients

  • Clinically significant active bleeding

  • Hepatic disease associated with coagulopathy and clinically relevant bleeding risk.

  • Lesion or condition, if considered to be a significant risk for major bleeding. This may include current or recent gastrointestinal ulceration, presence of malignant neoplasms at high risk of bleeding, recent brain or spinal injury, recent brain, spinal or ophthalmic surgery, recent intracranial haemorrhage, known or suspected oesophageal varices, arteriovenous malformations, vascular aneurysms or major intraspinal or intracerebral vascular abnormalities

  • Uncontrolled severe hypertension

  • Pregnancy and breast-feeding

  • Patients with severe renal impairment – Creatinine clearance <15ml/min

  • Prosthetic heart valves requiring anticoagulant treatment

  • Thrombophilias including antiphospholipid syndrome

  • Moderate to severe mitral stenosis

  • Active malignancy/chemotherapy (unless advised by a specialist)

  • Obesity

  • INR range greater than 2-3

  • Prescribed interacting drugs – see individual SPCs

Sources available include DAWN-AC, Dart, ICE, CED. The reviewing pharmacist will make a clinical decision (supported by use of risk scoring tools – see Appendix 1) – if in doubt, consult haematologist.

Baseline bloods – see Appendix 2

For patients with creatinine clearance 15-30ml/min, NOACs can be used, however, a 3 month blood test is recommended after switching, therefore consider impact on phlebotomy service

  1. Patient has been deemed eligible for switch to NOAC:



  • Contact patient by phone

  • If patient lacks capacity then involve GP/carer

  • Gain consent from patient to switch

  • Utilise regional decision making tool (click here) for excluding patient switch for absolute contra-indications only. Risk assess and mitigate any relative contra-indications whilst undertaking switch.

  • Check bloods (see Appendix 2)

  • Confirm patient weight

  • Discuss switch reasons and options (see appendix 7) – signpost to relevant information sources and supply electronically if required

  • Issue prescription for initial hospital supply from pharmacy via JAC pharmacy system, unverify prescription on JAC and ensure prescription is screened by a second pharmacist

  • Issue patient information booklets (found on anticoagulation desk) and send with prescription

  • Organise courier via dispensary to deliver medicines on the next working day at 11am. If this falls on a Saturday, ensure courier is available*

  • Complete template letter on CED for GP and supply to nominated community pharmacy using PharmOutcomes

  • Offer patient dedicated helpline to contact if issues (01702 385197)

  • Book patient in for 6 week follow up (via telephone consultation) with anticoagulant pharmacist. If no issues/concerns then patient discharged to GP.

  • Inform community nursing teams – contact domiciliary team

  • Liaise with anticoagulation pharmacist to update DAWN records and deactivate the account



*For switches, a standard 3 day break from warfarin is to be implemented prior to commencing NOAC

Patients refusing to review/change anticoagulation therapy:

  • They need to be informed of the significant safety risks associated with this and arrange a follow up call dependent on current phlebotomy service capacity

  • Complete template letter on CED for GP (see Appendix 6)

Appendix 2: Screening Bloods



  1. U&E's – Baseline bloods up to 6 months is suitable if Creatinine is within normal limits, patient clinically stable and low risk for change (consider age, comorbidities, previous AKI, current CKD, diabetes, NSAID use and drugs affecting renal function). Beyond this a repeat blood test is required.



  1. LFTs – Baseline bloods up to 1 year is suitable. Beyond this a repeat blood test is required or new baseline may be needed if suspicion for change e.g pre-existing liver or repeated disease, any new medications started that may derange LFTs, symptoms not investigated that could indicate liver problem.



Patients with elevated liver enzymes (ALT/AST > 2 x ULN) or total bilirubin ≥ 1.5 x ULN – refer to haematology for further discussion.



  1. FBC - not required blood test unless indicated – i.e. patient presents with signs of bleeding

    • If PLT <100 – GP to monitor every 6 months

    • If PLT <75 - continue anticoagulation but check CED if known explanation; if no explanation /unknown cause refer to haematology

    • If PLT <50 – anticoagulation contraindicated and refer to haematology if this is unexplained



    • If Hb <100 – GP to monitor every 6 months

    • If Hb < 90 - check if already investigated; if not refer to haematology.

Appendix 3: Bleeding Definitions for Anticoagulated Patients





The following definitions are from the ISTH (International Society of Thrombosis & Haemostasis) Guidelines



Term

Definition

Major Bleed

Clinically overt, including any one or more of:

    1. Bleeding resulting in decrease of ≥20g/L Hb in 24hrs

    2. Bleeding requiring transfusion of ≥2 units RBC

    3. Bleeding in critical site (intracranial, intraspinal, intraocular, pericardial, intra-articular, intramuscular + compartment syndrome, retroperitoneal)

    4. Bleeding that leads to death (but see ‘fatal bleed’)

CRNM Bleed

(Clinically relevant, non-major)

Clinically overt bleeding that does NOT satisfy any criteria for major bleeding, but leads to either:

    1. Hospital admission for bleeding

    2. Physician-guided medical/surgical treatment for bleeding

    3. A change in antithrombotic therapy

Minor Bleed

All clinically overt bleeding not meeting the criteria for Major or CRNM Bleeding.

Fatal Bleed

Bleed that directly contributes to death, or is the primary cause of death.

Appendix 7 -

The following are the issues to be covered during patient consultation on behalf of Dr xxx

Dear Patient,

Following the severe Covid 19 restrictions placed on your ability to leave your residence, we recognise how difficult it may be for you to have regular INR blood tests to monitor your warfarin treatment.

Unfortunately there are also fewer blood sampling sites and phlebotomists, so many patients are finding it harder to have their blood tested safely. Please bear in mind that it is not safe to take warfarin without regular INR blood tests.

We propose you can be safely converted to an alternative tablet, from a group of medicines commonly known as NOACs (also known as DOACs), which will continue your anticoagulant treatment, without the need for such regular blood test monitoring.

NOACs can be started three days after stopping warfarin. You will continue to be anticoagulated while on a NOAC so you must still remain careful to avoid injury, falling and excess alcohol. Most patients prefer NOACs to warfarin as no INR blood testing will be needed. There are no dietary restrictions or very few other drug interactions when taking a NOAC - which is a distinct advantage compared to warfarin.

We have many years’ experience in prescribing NOACs for our patients, converting from warfarin. NOACs are clinically proven to be equally effective to warfarin in clinical trials.Very few people have allergic reactions to NOACs but this is a small possibility. Just like warfarin, you must take the NOAC tablets regularly as prescribed.

There is less risk of serious bleeding overall while on a NOAC compared to a severe bleed while on warfarin in population based studies. One small drawback however, is that it is not as easy to reverse the effect of a NOAC, compared to warfarin, in case of a severe bleed.

After your first month's supply from the hospital, your GP will take over the prescribing who will be informed of the switch. Nearly all GPs are now experienced in prescribing NOACs and there are many hundreds of patients in our hospital service now on NOACs.

We require your consent to make the switch from Warfarin to a NOAC, but we strongly recommend that you do this, as long as you have good kidney function and are less than 100 kg (equivalent to 15 stone, 11 pounds) body weight.

This proposal has been approved by your anticoagulation pharmacists, consultant haematologists, GP representatives and the commissioners.
