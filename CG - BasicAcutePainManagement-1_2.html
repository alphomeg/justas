ACUTE PAIN SCORING

The clinician, the registered nurse, and all other healthcare providers have a responsibility to work collaboratively in assessing each individual patients needs in order to establish and implement a safe and appropriate pain management plan to provide optimum pain relief.

The below pain scoring tools are to be used with the modified World Health Organisation (WHO) Analgesic ladder (found on the NEWS observation chart). The ladder is designed to facilitate the consideration of pharmacological and non-pharmacological techniques for the relief of pain.

All adult patients admitted to the University Hospitals Bristol and Weston NHS Foundation Trust Hospitals will have their pain assessed, monitored and recorded using the Pain Assessment Tools provided in the UHBW NEWS chart.

Visual/Verbal Analogue Scale (VAS) for the cognitively able. The patient will be asked to score their pain, at rest and on movement, from 0 (no pain) to10 (severe pain).

The Abbey Scale for all patients who are unable to clearly articulate their needs e.g. patients with a diagnosis of dementia (any stage) or any other cognitive impairment. The patient is observed and scored 0 - 3 in six elements at rest and on movement. The scores in the six elements are added to represent a total pain score.

For full prescribing details and interactions, please refer to British National Formulary (BNF) or the Electronic

Medicines Compendium; Summary Product Characteristics (SPC) for further information.

Review daily and change to oral when appropriate.

GENERAL OPIOID INFORMATION

 Regular or high-dose opioids may cause reduced respiratory drive. This is a clinical risk for any patient, especially those with pre-existing respiratory failure +/- long-term domiciliary oxygen therapy.

 The dosage for the elderly should be substantially lower than for younger patients, it is generally safer practice to start with about 25-50% of the adult dose. Closely monitor for adverse effects and/or opioid toxicity.

 If concerned about respiratory depression and/or sedation, please increase frequency of observations on the NEWS chart. This allows monitoring of symptoms of opioid toxicity and common side effects.

** When prescribing analgesia for patients with/suspected hip femoral fragility fractures refer to: page 12, Femoral fragility fracture or suspected femoral fracture admission proforma for guidance on analgesia.

 In patients with renal impairment, please refer to the Clinical Guideline: Analgesic Prescribing in Adults with Renal Impairment.

 Pre-emptive regular laxatives e.g. Senna, Docusate Sodium and/or Macrogol should be prescribed if a patient is prescribed weak and/or strong opioids.

National Patient Safety Agency (2008). Reducing dosing errors with opioid medicines.

When prescribing, dispensing or administering strong opioids the healthcare practitioner should:

 Confirm any recent opioid dose, formulation, frequency of administration and any other analgesic medicines prescribed for the patient.

 Check starting dose, frequency of administration, standard dosing increments.

 Ensure where a dose increase is intended, that the calculated dose is safe for the patient.

Titrate the dose to achieve pain relief, increasing the dose by no more than 30-50% of the previous dose every 24-48 hours as needed to get pain under control (N.B. this excludes end of life patients).

NALOXONE

Required for all patients receiving opioids via IV or neuraxial route.

Consider Naloxone prescription when prescribing any weak/strong opioids especially for patients with renal
impairment and/or the elderly.

IV

Respiratory Depression (respiratory rate 8 or below).

Dilute 400 micrograms (1ml) of Naloxone in 3 ml Normal Saline 0.9% to a concentration of 100 micrograms per ml.

Administer 100 micrograms (1ml) IV. Repeat at 2 minute intervals until respiratory rate 12 or above per minute and sedation score is 2

or less.

Prior to discharge from recovery to ward, all postoperative patients must have prescribed:

 An appropriate analgesic regime including a strong opioid PRN for severe breakthrough pain (e. g. Morphine Sulphate oral solution) unless day-case patient or a strong opioid is contraindicated.

 Naloxone if patient is prescribed: Patient Controlled Analgesia (PCA), Epidural Fentanyl or if an intrathecal opioid has been used.

 Antiemetics e.g. Ondansetron, Prochlorperazine or Cyclizine.

 NB: Pre-printed prescription labels can be found in BRI: Hey groves theatres recovery, QDU recovery, general ITU and also in StMH recovery/theatres.
